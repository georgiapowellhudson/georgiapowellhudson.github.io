He said, she said, Chat GPT said

The rise of artificial intelligence hallucinations, fabrications in litigation

By Georgia Hudson

In 2022, Roberto Mata filed a personal injury case against Avianca, Inc, claiming that while on a flight with the airline, a beverage cart struck his knee. The case became a landmark decision, but it had nothing to do with Mata or his alleged injury.
His representation, Steven A. Schwartz, a lawyer from Tribeca firm Levidow, Levidow & Oberman P.C, had used Chat GPT in his brief to the Southern District of New York. The document cited more than a half a dozen cases that never existed.
Judge P. Kevin Castel dismissed the case and ordered Schwartz to pay a $5,000 fine. Castel’s opinion is referenced by judges and attorneys across the country today, as he defined the professional liability of employing generative tools in the courtroom.
“Technological advances are commonplace,” Castel said in his Opinion and Order on Sanctions. “And there is nothing inherently improper about using a reliable artificial intelligence tool for assistance. But existing rules impose a gatekeeping role on attorneys to ensure the accuracy of their filings.”
Mata V. Avianca Inc. is among 1334 instances of artificial intelligence hallucinations recorded in Damien Charlotin’s database. Alongside other lawyers and legal professionals, he’s working to provide empirical evidence of examples of AI fabrications in court decisions.
Of the 1334, 662 occurrences involve Pro Se Litigants, meaning individuals representing themselves in court. Jesse A. Schaefer, an attorney with Womble Bond Dickinson in North Carolina, attributes that to accessibility and affordability.
In an interview with the New York Times in Nov. of 2025, he said that some plaintiffs and defendants are turning to chat bots in favor of hiring lawyers. For all its faults, he added that AI can bridge the diction gap between legalists and the public.
Syracuse Law teaching professor Jack Graves led the college’s first AI Residency program in Aug. 2025, where law students around the country participated in a week-long exploration of the effects of the technology on the legal field. He also teaches The Intersection of Law and Artificial Intelligence.
Graves added that without educational and professional avenues like the ones the college offers, hallucinations will only continue.
“I think the hallucination issue is mischaracterized,” Graves said. “I don't think hallucinations represent a problem with AI, I think they represent a problem with lawyers and law students. No lawyer should ever rely on a case without verifying it.”
According to the data, the large majority, 1127, of these instances revolve around fabrications, meaning a language learning model is entirely making up its references. Case Law was the most hallucinated material, with 1076 infractions, meaning that tools like Chat GPT and Claude are providing litigants with false precedents, or old cases.
Fabricated case law was the infraction in Mata V. Avianca, Inc.
Schwartz’s brief had several citations of past decisions in personal injury lawsuits against airlines, including Varghese v. China Southern Airlines. The case not only didn’t contain the information it was cited to have, it never existed at all.
“Humans make mistakes, so does AI.” Graves said. “Failing to vet either is inexcusable if you're a lawyer. So I don't see this as an AI problem. I see it's a human problem.”
Graves' opinion is in line with the American Bar Association, whose Standing Committee On Ethics and Professional Responsibility published their first formal opinion on Generative Artificial Intelligence Tools in Jul. 2024.

“To ensure clients are protected, lawyers using generative artificial intelligence tools must fully consider their applicable ethical obligations, including their duties to provide competent legal representation, to protect client information…,” Formal Opinion 512 said.
Mentioned in the opinion, a unique aspect of the use of generative ai in the legal profession is the impact the technology has on attorney client privilege. As both professionals and Pro Se Litigents turn to language learning models for research, they have the capacity to plug in pertinent case information that would violate confidentiality.
Graves said that the court rulings on that, so far, are not consistent.
“The short, simple answer comes down to whether we treat AI as a communicative tool, or whether we treat it like we treat a person,” Graves said. “Many people use AI like a legal assistant. If you were to do that and treat it as a person, then as long as you're doing something at the request of counsel as part of the representative relationship, I think there's an argument for privilege.”
Like the gray area of confidentiality, within the data, there is little uniformity in punishment. One fabrication, Bunce v. Visual Technology Innovations (2026), resulted in a $5,000 fine for fabrication and misrepresentation.
Another, Cynthia White v. Walmart, Inc. (2026), was closed by the judge with a warning for those same infractions.
